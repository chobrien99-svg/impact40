# France Just Named Its 120 Most Promising Impact Startups — And the List Says a Lot About Where the Country Is Heading

On March 31, 2026, Impact France — the country's leading organization for socially and environmentally engaged entrepreneurs — unveiled its third annual Impact 40/120 index, a ranking of the 120 French startups most likely to become the next generation of "impact unicorns." The structure is a two-tier system: the top 40 companies deemed closest to unicorn status form the elite Impact 40, while 80 more high-potential companies round out the Impact 120. Published in partnership with the business daily Les Echos, the list reads like a roadmap of France's economic ambitions: cleaner energy, a circular economy, AI with a conscience, and a healthcare system that actually reaches people who need it.

And the numbers backing the cohort are striking. The laureates have collectively raised 7.4 billion euros since inception — a 17.5% jump from 2024. More than half (53%) now operate internationally. A full 51% have at least one woman on the founding team. And 36% are already profitable, a figure that quietly demolishes the old cliché that purpose-driven companies can't make money.

## The Circular Economy Is King

The single biggest sector in the 2026 cohort? Circular economy and sustainable consumption, with at least 28 companies. That's not a niche — it's a movement. Names like Too Good To Go (the anti-food-waste giant), Murfy (which repairs rather than replaces home appliances), 900.care (refillable personal care), and CertiDeal (refurbished electronics) have turned reducing waste into scalable business models. Le Fourgon, out of northern France, revives the old-fashioned returnable bottle — and has grown into a 200-plus-person operation doing it.

Within the elite Impact 40 tier — the top 40 companies considered most likely to reach unicorn status — eight are in circular economy. France is clearly betting that the future of consumption isn't about buying more, but buying smarter.

## Energy Transition Takes Center Stage

Energy is the second strongest sector in the Impact 40, with seven companies in the top tier. Verkor, the Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes-based battery manufacturer with 500+ employees, is France's answer to Europe's dependence on Asian battery supply chains. Electra is rolling out ultra-fast EV charging stations. Elyse Energy is developing low-carbon molecules. Qair, another 500+ employee company, is a renewable energy producer. Hello Watt helps households slash their energy bills. The message is clear: France isn't just talking about energy sovereignty — it's funding it.

## AI Arrives, But With a Twist

For the first time, AI has emerged as a major standalone category, with 12 companies across the full cohort. But these aren't your typical Silicon Valley AI plays. Implicity uses AI to monitor cardiac implants remotely. Kayrros deploys satellite imagery and machine learning to track methane emissions globally. The cohort's AI companies exist not to disrupt for disruption's sake, but to solve specific, measurable problems in health and climate. Even the French government noticed: Anne Le Hénanff, the Minister for AI and Digital, personally endorsed the cohort, noting that impact startups are "key to addressing the strategic challenges of energy transition, generational change, and social cohesion."

## Healthcare and Aging: France's Demographic Reckoning

With 13 companies in health and vulnerability, and 12 more in social inclusion, the cohort reveals a country grappling with its aging population. Groupe Ouihelp, Alenvi, CetteFamille, Groupe MonSenior, and NEOSILVER are all building infrastructure to care for elderly people at home or in community settings. Hublo tackles healthcare staffing shortages. May supports new parents' mental health. This isn't just entrepreneurship — it's demographic triage. France has one of Europe's fastest-aging populations, and these startups are building the systems the state hasn't.

## Paris Dominates, But Not Entirely

The majority of laureates are based in Île-de-France — no surprise there. But the regional spread tells an interesting story. Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes claims nine laureates, including the industrial heavyweights Verkor and Elyse Energy. Pays de la Loire, Occitanie, and Nouvelle-Aquitaine each contribute six. Even France's overseas territories are represented, with Domissori, an education startup, based in Outre-mer. The geography suggests that while Paris remains the gravitational center, impact entrepreneurship is genuinely decentralizing.

## What the Numbers Reveal About Larger Trends

Step back and the 2026 cohort tells a bigger story — about France, about Europe, and about capitalism itself.

First, the "impact unicorn" concept is maturing. The BCG-Impact France-ESSEC research underlying the index has developed a methodology that values companies not just by financial returns but by "costs avoided" for society — reduced CO2 emissions, lower healthcare spending, fewer people in poverty. Their studies show that impact startups generate social and environmental value equivalent to 130% of their revenue. That's a paradigm shift in how we define corporate success, and it's happening in France first.

Second, the strong representation of circular economy and energy companies reflects Europe's regulatory environment. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is forcing every large company to account for its social and environmental footprint. French impact startups aren't just benefiting from this — they're the ones building the tools and infrastructure that make compliance possible.

Third, the gender numbers matter. Having 51% of laureates with a female co-founder is remarkable by any country's standards, and suggests that impact entrepreneurship is opening doors that traditional tech has kept firmly shut.

Finally, the profitability figure — 36% of these startups are already in the black — is the quiet headline. For years, impact investing was treated as a charitable exercise. The 2026 Impact 40/120 cohort suggests something different: that solving society's hardest problems might actually be good business. In a France squeezed by budget austerity and social tension, that's not just encouraging. It's essential.
